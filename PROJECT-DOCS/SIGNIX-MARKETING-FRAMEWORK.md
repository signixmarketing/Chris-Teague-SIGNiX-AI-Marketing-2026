## 1. The Framework We Use

The Teague Marketing Framework organizes all marketing decisions around four dimensions:

- **Marketing Objective:** Acquire new customers or Retain existing ones
- **Customer Type:** Direct (buys SIGNiX) or Indirect (accesses SIGNiX through a partner)
- **Customer Value:** Loyalist (active, high-transaction) or Dabbler (low or no transaction activity)
- **Target Audience:** Buyer, Influencer, or User

These four dimensions sit inside a 2x2 strategic model:

| | **Acquisition** | **Retention** |
|---|---|---|
| **Competitive Focus** | Attract new category users. Stimulate demand. Convert competitive users. | — |
| **Customer Focus** | Earn share from multi-vendor accounts. | Stimulate more use. Add more value. |

The Big Picture Partners 6Bs model sits underneath this. Every go-to-market decision maps to six variables: Brand, Business category, Bodies, Beliefs, Behaviors, and Benchmarks.

Reference: [bigpicturepartners.com/framework](https://bigpicturepartners.com/framework/)

## 2. Go-to-Market Strategy Declaration

SIGNiX runs two strategies in parallel, confirmed by the CEO in April 2026:

**Strategy 1: Brand Commitment (Depth lever)**
For existing TruStage FI customers who are dabblers — they have SIGNiX available but use zero authentications. The goal is to move them from low-activity users to active authentication users. This is retention-focused and grows transaction revenue without acquiring new customers.

**Strategy 2: Brand Switching + Category Development (Width lever)**
For new-to-SIGNiX prospects in five target segments where legal or regulatory authority requires signatures that hold up as verified proof. In most cases, these prospects already use DocuSign or Adobe Sign. The goal is to give them a reason to switch — or to enter the category for the first time (integration partners with no e-sign today).

> In plain terms: Depth is about getting more from customers we already have. Width is about winning accounts from competitors and building new channels.

## 3. The 6Bs Applied to SIGNiX

### Brand

SIGNiX's brand position: the e-sign platform you trust when the signature has to hold up.

The core positioning statement to test:
> "When your signature gets challenged in a FINRA exam, a HIPAA audit, or a collection dispute, SIGNiX gives you a person who knows your workflow and a document that proves who signed."

Two things that separate SIGNiX from DocuSign and Adobe Sign:
1. PKI-based digital signatures bind a verified identity to a document. A click-to-sign image does not.
2. SIGNiX assigns a CSM who stays involved after the sale. DocuSign puts you in a ticket queue.

Fear opens the door. Partnership closes the deal.

**Note on AI disruption:** AI is making e-signatures more dangerous, not less. Deepfakes, synthetic identities, and AI-generated documents are increasing fraud risk across every industry. SIGNiX's PKI and identity verification model is more resistant to these threats than standard e-sign. This is the basis of the CEO's request to educate senior leadership on AI risk.

---

### Business Category

SIGNiX has spent 20 years trying to define itself as a "digital signature" company, distinct from "e-signature." That argument has not driven mass market growth. Most buyers do not search for the distinction.

What does drive growth: the specific problem each buyer is trying to solve. A FINRA compliance officer is not searching for "digital signature." They are searching for "FINRA compliant e-signature audit trail." A legal notice publisher is not searching for "PKI." They are searching for "remote online notary for legal notices."

The category SIGNiX should own in buyers' minds:
> Signing that holds up. For industries where proof of identity is not optional.

For the integration partner audience, the category is different:
> An e-sign API that gives your clients a compliance-grade option your competitors do not offer.

### Bodies (Five Target Segments)

#### Segment 1: Wealth Management and Broker-Dealers
**Examples:** Pershing (BNY Mellon), Osaic, Docupace
**Customer Type:** Direct and indirect (Docupace is an integration partner)
**Customer Value:** Loyalist (Pershing, Osaic are active; Docupace embeds SIGNiX)
**Target Audience:**
- Buyer: VP Operations, Chief Compliance Officer, Director of Technology
- Influencer: Docupace product team (for indirect customers)
- User: Advisor support staff, back office operations

**Authority:** FINRA and SEC. Documents like beneficiary changes, wire authorizations, IRA distributions, and account transfers must be provable under audit. A click does not prove identity. A PKI signature does.

**Strategy:** Brand switching (from DocuSign/Adobe) for new prospects. Brand expansion (deeper integration) for Docupace.

---

#### Segment 2: Credit Unions and Banks via TruStage
**Examples:** FIs in the TruStage loan document network
**Customer Type:** Indirect (SIGNiX via TruStage chain: FI → CSI/Hawthorn River LOS → TruStage → SIGNiX)
**Customer Value:** Majority are dabblers — signing but using zero authentications
**Target Audience:**
- Buyer: VP of Lending, VP of Operations, IT Director
- Influencer: TruStage CSMs, CSI/Hawthorn River account managers
- User: Loan officers, member-facing staff

**Authority:** NCUA, banking regulators, CFPB. Fraud risk is the near-term trigger. A member who disputes a loan signature and cannot be proven to have signed is both a financial and regulatory exposure.

**Strategy:** Brand commitment. These FIs already have SIGNiX. The job is activation, not acquisition.

---

#### Segment 3: Debt Buyers, Servicers, and Collection Firms
**Examples:** Cavalry Portfolio Services, InvestiNet, Aristocrat Process Serving
**Customer Type:** Direct
**Customer Value:** New customers — dabblers to be acquired
**Target Audience:**
- Buyer: VP Operations, General Counsel, Director of Collections
- Influencer: Collection software platform vendors (e.g., Convoke)
- User: Collections staff, process servers

**Authority:** Courts, FDCPA, state civil procedure rules. Chain of title documents must be provable. Payment agreements must be defensible. Process service affidavits often require notarization.

**Strategy:** Brand switching (most have DocuSign or no e-sign) and category development (RON for process servers is a new capability many do not know SIGNiX offers).

---

#### Segment 4: Media and Legal Publishers
**Examples:** Advance Local, Gannett / USA TODAY Co.
**Customer Type:** Direct and indirect (Gannett via Lineup integration)
**Customer Value:** New customers — high volume RON use case
**Target Audience:**
- Buyer: VP Operations, Legal Counsel, Publisher
- Influencer: Lineup / Chartbeat product team (for Gannett)
- User: Legal notice coordinators, editorial operations

**Authority:** State laws requiring notarization for public legal notices — foreclosures, probate filings, court proceedings. High volume, recurring, legally mandated. DocuSign's SignRequest (the current Lineup incumbent) cannot do RON.

**Strategy:** Category development (many publishers do not know RON is an option) and brand switching from SignRequest/DocuSign for ad contracts.

**Note on Lineup:** Gannett has already integrated Lineup into their ad sales workflow. The incumbent e-sign solution inside Lineup is SignRequest, which DocuSign acquired in 2021. SignRequest is click-to-sign only. It cannot do RON. If SIGNiX integrates with Lineup, it replaces SignRequest for both advertising contracts and legal notice notarization.

---

#### Segment 5: Software Integration Partners
**Examples:** Docupace (wealth management), Convoke (debt recovery), Lineup (media ad sales)
**Customer Type:** Indirect (partners who embed SIGNiX in their platforms)
**Customer Value:** High strategic value — one integration multiplies SIGNiX's reach across many end customers
**Target Audience:**
- Buyer: Chief Product Officer, VP of Product, CTO
- Influencer: Their key enterprise clients who are asking for better identity verification
- User: The end-customer's signing staff

**Authority:** Varies by platform's industry. Compliance demands from the partner's clients create the internal case for the integration.

**Strategy:** Brand expansion. These partners already work with SIGNiX or are open to it. The job is to deepen the integration, grow adoption across their client base, and block competitors from entering through that same door.

## 5. Competitive Landscape

| Competitor | Their Strength | Their Weakness | SIGNiX Counter |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|----------------|
| DocuSign | Brand recognition, broad integrations, ease of use | Click-to-sign cannot prove identity. No dedicated CSM. SignRequest (their RON attempt) cannot do full RON. | "A click does not prove who signed. SIGNiX does." |
| Adobe Sign | Existing enterprise relationships and bundled pricing | No RON capability. No identity verification beyond click. | "Adobe sells software. We assign a person." |
| PandaDoc | Easy proposal and contract signing for SMBs | No regulated industry focus, no authentication, no RON | Not a direct competitor in our target segments — buyers who choose PandaDoc are not yet our buyers |
| SignRequest (DocuSign-owned, in Lineup) | Already embedded in Lineup / Gannett workflow | Cannot do RON. Click-to-sign only. | "Your legal notice volume requires notarization. SignRequest cannot do that. SIGNiX can." |
| Notarize / Proof | Consumer-facing RON convenience | Not built for regulated B2B workflows or high-volume legal publishers | "Built for consumer convenience, not regulated industries at scale." |

**The threat we must watch:** Large customers building their own solution (the Sarah Cannon pattern). The deeper SIGNiX is embedded in a customer's workflow via the Flex API, the harder it is to replace or rebuild. API depth is the best retention tool.
