Last November, a jury convicted a woman for using false documents and aliases to take $75,000 from Dime Bank in New York. She visited multiple branches under different names. The bank approved the loan. No one could verify who was actually signing the documents.

A conviction is a good outcome. But the bank still lost time and staff resources. And no one could say with certainty who authorized those documents. Here is the case.

Uniondale Woman Found Guilty in $75K Loan Scam (Long Island Business News, November 2025)

The defendant visited multiple Dime Bank branches using different names and other individuals posing as business owners. She submitted forged corporate documents and a forged bank statement. One $75,000 loan was approved. The fraud worked across multiple branch visits before investigators identified the pattern.

The bank's branch manager visited the listed business address and found nothing. But by the time investigators confirmed the fraud, the money was gone. The bank had no verified identity attached to the documents at the point of signing. Anyone could submit paperwork. No one could prove who authorized it.
